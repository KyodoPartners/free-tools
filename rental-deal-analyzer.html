* Why your monthly cost rises over the years even though the mortgage doesn’t: Your mortgage principal & interest (P&I) is fixed for the life of a fixed-rate loan — it never changes here. What rises is everything else: property tax, insurance, maintenance, HOA and other running costs (they grow at your “your costs go up %/yr” rate), plus property management, which is a % of rent so it climbs as rent climbs. These grow by compounding — each year’s increase stacks on the previous year’s total, not on the starting amount. At 2%/yr that’s about +22% after 10 years and roughly +80% after 30 (1.02^30 ≈ 1.81), not a flat 2% × 30 = 60%. Because the fixed P&I is usually your biggest line, your total payment rises much more slowly than that — and when the loan is paid off at the end of its term, P&I drops to $0, so the monthly cost falls sharply that year.

Plain-English glossary — Cap rate: the property's yearly income (after running costs, before the mortgage) ÷ its price — its "yield." 4%+ is healthy; expensive markets run ~1%. Cash-on-cash (CoC): your yearly cash flow ÷ the cash you put in — the return on your actual dollars; 8%+ is good. Cash flow: rent minus every cost including the mortgage — what lands in (or leaves) your pocket. DSCR: does the rent cover the mortgage? 1.0 = exactly; lenders like 1.20+. NOI: yearly income after running costs, before the mortgage. P&I: the principal + interest part of your monthly mortgage (taxes & insurance are billed separately). Equity: the home's value minus what you still owe — what you'd keep if you sold and paid off the loan. Appreciation (default 4%/yr) is a market assumption, not a guarantee.

Plain-English glossary — ARV (after-repair value): what you expect to sell for once the repairs are done. Repairs / rehab to sell: what you spend fixing it up to sell — itemize it in the repairs section above. Holding cost: what it costs to own it while you fix and sell — the monthly loan payment (principal & interest), property tax, insurance and any other carry, times the months you hold. Loan payoff: the balance you still owe the lender, paid off at the sale. Cash from sale: the resale price minus agent/closing costs and the loan payoff. Profit: cash from sale minus your cash in (down + closing + rehab) minus the holding cost. ROI: profit ÷ the cash you put in. Annualized return: that ROI expressed per year for how long you held it.

Plain-English glossary — Cap rate: the ADU's yearly income after its running costs ÷ its build cost — its all-cash yield. Cash-on-cash (CoC): the ADU's yearly cash flow ÷ the cash you put in. Cash flow/mo & /yr: the ADU's rent minus every cost it adds (and the build-loan payment, if you financed it) — what it puts in your pocket. DSCR (shown only if you took a build loan): the ADU's income after costs ÷ its loan payment; over 1.0 the rent covers the loan. This view models only the ADU's cost and your lot — not your home — so it can't estimate a sale.

Plain-English glossary — Pay it yourself: what you'd pay each month to live here with no renter — your mortgage (principal & interest) plus the whole-house bills (tax, insurance, upkeep) you'd pay regardless. With a renter: the same, minus what renting the space saves you. Gain/mo & Gain/yr: how much renting cuts your out-of-pocket each month (× 12 for the year). Equity: the home's value minus what you still owe — what you'd keep if you sold and paid off the loan. Appreciation (default 4%/yr) is a market assumption, not a guarantee. Your mortgage is housing you'd pay either way, so it's never shown as a loss.

ADU notes: the all-in build cost should include commonly-missed extras — utility hookups/upgrades, site prep, design & permits, impact fees (waived for CA ADUs under 750 sq ft), and possible fire sprinklers. Lot fit (California): no minimum lot size; detached ADUs can sit ~4 ft from side/rear lines; ADUs ≤800 sq ft are exempt from lot-coverage limits. The fit check is a rough guide — confirm with your city.
