Estimates for planning only — not investment, tax or accounting advice. Figures depend on the numbers you enter; verify locally.

Investment notes: Cap rate uses operating expenses only (excludes the mortgage). Maintenance is a fixed $/yr (default ≈1% of value). Appreciation (default 4%/yr) is a market assumption, not a GAAP/accounting rule — long-run U.S. home prices rose ~4–4.3%/yr nominally (FHFA/Case-Shiller, 1975–2025); the Bay Area has been higher but more volatile. Resale price (optional): if you enter one, every "sold" figure starts from it (grown by your appreciation rate) instead of purchase price + appreciation — use it for a flip or value-add where you know the likely sale value.

Owner-occupy notes: the monthly cash flow is your net housing cost — what you pay after the renter helps — so a negative number is normal and expected; you cover the rest from your job income. The real payoff is wealth: a renter pays down your mortgage and you keep the appreciation, shown in "Total profit if sold." Cap rate, cash-on-cash and DSCR are investment yardsticks and will read low because only the rented portion counts as income.

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) answers one question: does the property's income cover the mortgage payment? It is net operating income (rent minus operating expenses, before the mortgage) ÷ the annual mortgage payment. 1.0 = income exactly covers the loan payment; above 1.0 = income more than covers it (lenders typically want 1.20–1.25 or higher); below 1.0 = income does not cover the loan, so you feed it cash each month.

The other metrics, plain-English: Cap rate = yearly net operating income ÷ purchase price — the property's yield before the mortgage; 4%+ is healthy, under ~3% is thin (Bay Area buy-to-rent is often ~1%). CoC (cash-on-cash) = yearly cash flow ÷ the cash you put in (down payment + closing + remodel) — your return on actual dollars invested; 8%+ is good. Cash flow/mo = rent minus all operating costs and the mortgage (what lands in, or leaves, your pocket). ROI/sqft = yearly cash flow ÷ total square footage. P&I/mo = principal + interest — your fixed monthly mortgage payment (tax & insurance billed separately).

Quick ratios — what they mean & what's "good": Rent/sqft = monthly rent ÷ square footage; higher means more income per foot — useful to compare similar properties, no universal target. Value/sqft = price ÷ square footage; compare to recent comparable sales to judge if you're overpaying. 1% rule = monthly rent ÷ price; ≥ 1% traditionally signals a property can cash-flow — the Bay Area runs ~0.3% because prices far outrun rents (a flashing "won't cash-flow on rent alone" sign). NOI (net operating income) = yearly rent minus operating expenses, before the mortgage; it's what's left to cover the loan and your profit — higher is better, and it's the number a cap rate is built from.

The stress test is a one-year snapshot: it re-runs the deal with the bad-year shocks you set (rent drop, extra vacancy, rate rise) — each on its own, then all three together in the last row. All three rarely hit at once, so the "all 3" row is a true worst case. A negative number means that year you'd feed the property cash out of pocket; the bigger the cushion above $0, the safer the deal.

ADU notes: the build cost should include commonly-missed extras — utility hookups/upgrades (~$10–35K), site prep/foundation/grading, design & permits, impact fees (waived for ADUs under 750 sq ft in CA; proportional above), and possible fire sprinklers. Lot fit (California): no minimum lot size for an ADU; detached ADUs can sit ~4 ft from side/rear lines; ADUs ≤800 sq ft are exempt from lot-coverage limits, larger ones must meet local coverage (often 30–50%). The fit check is a rough guide — confirm with your city.

How to read the ADU numbers: ADU cap rate = the ADU's yearly net income (rent after its vacancy, tax, insurance & maintenance) ÷ the all-in build cost — the return the build itself earns, before any loan. 4%+ is solid; 6–7% is strong, and it typically beats buying a whole separate house to rent in the Bay Area (~1%). Payback = the year the rent (after costs) has repaid the build — the single most important ADU number; under ~15 years is good, the lower the better. ROI/sqft = yearly net income ÷ ADU square footage; cost/sqft = build ÷ ADU sqft (Bay Area ADUs commonly run $300–600/sqft all-in); rent/sqft = monthly rent ÷ ADU sqft for comparing to local rentals.
